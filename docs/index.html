This document specifies the ODRL Legal Profile (ODRL-L), an ODRL 2.2 profile that gives an OWL vocabulary for the normative positions an ODRL policy brings into existence when it is in force. An ODRL evaluator returns a verdict (active, violated, fulfilled, not-set) but says nothing about who may, must, or can do what, in relation to whom, once a policy is active. The profile makes those positions explicit by grounding ODRL in the foundational ontology of legal relations UFO-L: each activated rule founds a simple legal relator bundling one correlative pair of legal positions, one position for the assignee and the matching correlative for the assigner.

The profile defines fifteen classes (one abstract position supertype, the eight UFO-L leaf positions, one abstract relator root, and five concrete relator classes) and eight properties. It surfaces the eight legal positions (Permission, No Right, Duty, Right, Power, Subjection, Immunity, Disability) from the two that are implicit in existing evaluators, adding in particular the competence-level Power–Subjection authority to declare a violation. Two extension points, odrl-l:hasRemedy and odrl-l:stronglyPermitted, let policy authors signal competence-level structure at authoring time. The profile reuses ODRL [[ODRL-MODEL]] [[ODRL-VOCAB]] as its foundational vocabulary. It is an OWL approximation of a first-order grounding whose full semantics, axioms, and proofs are given in the companion paper [[UFOL-PAPER]] and mechanically verified with Isabelle/HOL, Vampire, E, and Z3.

This document is a working draft that specifies the ODRL Legal Profile (ODRL-L). It accompanies the paper What Does ODRL Mean? A Cross-Level Ontological Grounding of Permissions, Prohibitions, and Duties in UFO-L [[UFOL-PAPER]]. It is not a standards-track document of any organisation. Introduction

Context

The Open Digital Rights Language (ODRL) expresses permissions, prohibitions, and duties over assets, and has been adopted by data-space initiatives as a common language for usage and access policies. The ODRL Formal Semantics [[ODRL-FS]] specifies an ODRL Evaluator, a component that decides which rules are active and which actions are permitted, prohibited, fulfilled, or violated. Its output is a verdict.

A verdict does not say what normative situation a policy creates, nor what authority structures that situation presupposes. Consider an events portal, :KulturPortal, that obtains the same theatre showtimes from two providers, :BerlinerEnsemble and :DeutschesTheater, each under an odrl:Agreement that prohibits redistribution and attaches a remedy. When the portal redistributes, the evaluator returns violated for both policies but cannot say by whose authority either remedy is enforceable: the policy graph records the remedy assignees, but it has no term for the provider's Power to declare the violation, nor for the provider's correlative Right to demand omission. Those normative positions are entailed by the policy yet absent from both the evaluator and the graph.

Scope and approach

The profile grounds the three ODRL constructs that generate normative positions between parties, Permission, Prohibition, and Duty (Asset and Party are structural relata; Constraint is an activation condition that creates no position). Each activated rule is mapped to a UFO-L simple legal relator that bundles exactly one correlative pair of legal positions over the same content (an action type and a target). Positions are partitioned into two levels: the conduct level governs what agents may, must, or must not do (Permission–No Right, Right–Duty); the competence level governs the authority to bring positions into or out of existence through institutional acts (Power–Subjection, Immunity–Disability).

The profile governs the vocabulary (position classes, relator classes, and the properties that relate them) and provides an OWL surface for authors to assert competence-level structure. It does not redefine the ODRL constraint-evaluation semantics, nor introduce new operators. Because OWL lacks function symbols and is restricted to a decidable description-logic fragment, the profile is a deliberate approximation of the first-order grounding; the limits are stated in § OWL approximation limits.

Conformance criterion

A policy graph uses this profile conformantly when, for every legal position it asserts, the position is an instance of one of the eight leaf classes, inheres in a party through odrl-l:bearer, carries its content through odrl-l:normContent (and odrl:target), and is a part (through odrl-l:partOf) of a relator of the matching kind, with the bearer assignment (assignee or assigner) and the correlative pairing as documented in § Legal positions and § Relators. The two extension points, odrl-l:hasRemedy and odrl-l:stronglyPermitted, are asserted on ODRL rule nodes as defined in § Extension points. The correlativity and incompatibility constraints (Axioms 5.9 and 5.10 of [[UFOL-PAPER]]) are not enforceable in OWL and are checked in the first-order encodings of the benchmark (§ Verification).

Conceptual model

A legal position is a UFO-L legal moment: an existentially dependent particular that inheres in exactly one bearer (an agent). Positions are classified as Legal Entitlements (implying an advantage) or Legal Burdens/Lacks (implying a constraint), and always come in correlative pairs. A simple legal relator is the mereological sum of exactly one such pair: one position inhering in the assignee and the matching correlative inhering in the assigner. It captures a bilateral normative bond as a single individual, founded by a unique activation event.

odrl-l:LegalPosition is the abstract supertype of the eight leaf position classes. odrl-l:ODRLRelator is the abstract root of the five concrete relator classes; a relator is typed as an odrl-l:ODRLRelator when an ODRL rule of the matching kind founds it (Axiom 5.7 of [[UFOL-PAPER]]). The eight leaf positions are pairwise disjoint, as are the five concrete relator classes.

Each activated rule founds exactly one relator. A prohibition that carries a remedy additionally founds a competence-level remedy relator at the same event, and a strongly permitted permission additionally founds an immunity relator; the distinct founding properties keep these separate relators apart (Axiom 5.6 of [[UFOL-PAPER]]).

Notes on content. The odrl-l:Duty and odrl-l:Right arising from a prohibition are over the omission of the action (a Duty to Omit and a Right to Omission), whereas those arising from an obligation are over the action itself (a Duty to Act and a Right to Action); the relator class disambiguates the two cases. The content of odrl-l:Power and odrl-l:Subjection is the institutional act of declaring a violation. The forbearance and declaration functions are not OWL-representable (§ OWL approximation limits), so in this profile odrl-l:normContent carries the underlying action. odrl-l:Immunity is not exclusive to strong permission: it can arise from any source that constitutionally entrenches a position against revocation.

Extension points

The profile adds two terms (marked † above) that an author asserts on an ODRL rule to make competence-level structure explicit at authoring time.

odrl-l:hasRemedy is a sub-property of odrl:remedy: asserting it both records the structural ODRL remedy link and signals that activating the prohibition founds a odrl-l:RemedyRelator, giving the assigner the Power to declare a violation and the assignee the correlative Subjection.

odrl-l:stronglyPermitted is an annotation property on an odrl:Permission; its presence signals that the permission founds an odrl-l:ImmunityRelator alongside the permission relator, so the permission persists against a later prohibition. As an annotation property it carries no OWL entailment: founding the immunity relator requires a rule engine or a SPARQL construct over the policy graph. 

ex:p a odrl:Permission ;
    odrl:assigner ex:Provider ;
    odrl:assignee ex:Consumer ;
    odrl:action   odrl:read ;
    odrl:target   ex:Dataset ;
    odrl-l:stronglyPermitted true .

Formal semantics

This section summarises the grounding; the full development, axioms, and proofs are in the companion paper [[UFOL-PAPER]].

Rules as simple legal relators

Each activated ODRL rule founds exactly one simple legal relator that bundles one correlative pair of positions, one for the assignee and the matching correlative for the assigner, over the same action and target. This is the minimal adequate grounding: a single rule creates exactly one pair, and a simple legal relator bundles exactly one pair.

The Cross-Level Design Principle

A norm is violable if the regulated action remains performable despite the norm, and consequential if violation activates a reparative position. The central result of [[UFOL-PAPER]] is that any norm that is both violable and consequential has no adequate characterisation using conduct-level positions alone: grounding the step from violation to remedy requires a competence-level Power–Subjection pair. ODRL prohibition is sanctioned (the prohibited action is performable and its performance reaches violated), and a remedy is a consequence of violation by definition, so any complete grounding of ODRL requires competence-level positions. This is why the profile adds Power, Subjection, Immunity, and Disability to the conduct-level positions an evaluator already implies.

Soundness and incompleteness of the evaluator

Mapped onto the profile, an ODRL evaluator is sound but not complete: a permit verdict entails a Permission, a prohibition entails a Duty to Omit, and a prohibition with a remedy entails a Power, but the evaluator reports none of the four entailed correlatives (No Right, Right to Omission, Immunity, Disability). The profile makes all eight explicit.

OWL approximation limits

The profile is an OWL approximation of the first-order grounding, with three limits, all stemming from OWL's lack of function symbols and its decidable description-logic fragment.

  • Forbearance content is not representable. There is no OWL term for the omission of an action as content, so a Duty to Omit and a Right to Omission carry the underlying action through odrl-l:normContent; the forbearance character is captured only in the first-order encodings.
  • stronglyPermitted carries no entailment. It is an annotation flag: founding the immunity relator (Axiom 5.2) is a rule OWL cannot derive, so it requires a rule engine or a SPARQL construct.
  • Correlativity and incompatibility require full first-order logic. Axiom 5.9 (Correlativity, a unique-existence biconditional over shared content) and Axiom 5.10 (Normative Position Incompatibility, a contradiction over forbearance-related content) are not encoded in this OWL layer and are checked in the benchmark.

The ternary predicates founds/3 and cnt/3 are likewise approximated as binary OWL object properties; cnt/3 is split into odrl-l:normContent (action) and odrl:target (target).

Example

The Berliner Ensemble agreement of the introduction. The author writes odrl-l:hasRemedy in place of plain odrl:remedy, which signals that activation founds the competence relator that carries the violation-declaration authority the bare ODRL graph leaves implicit. 

ex:BE-Agreement a odrl:Agreement ;
    odrl:prohibition [
        odrl:assigner ex:BerlinerEnsemble ;
        odrl:assignee ex:KulturPortal ;
        odrl:action   odrl:distribute ;
        odrl:target   ex:BE-Showtimes ;
        odrl-l:hasRemedy [            # signals Ax 5.4: founds a RemedyRelator
            a             odrl:Duty ;
            odrl:action   odrl:compensate ;
            odrl:assignee ex:BerlinerEnsemble
        ]
    ] .

Activating this prohibition founds two relators at one event: a odrl-l:ProhibitionRelator (Duty to Omit for :KulturPortal, Right to Omission for :BerlinerEnsemble) and, because of odrl-l:hasRemedy, a odrl-l:RemedyRelator giving :BerlinerEnsemble the Power to declare a violation and :KulturPortal the correlative Subjection. Materialised with the profile's properties, the remedy relator is: 

ex:BE-Agreement-rhoR a odrl-l:RemedyRelator .

ex:BE-power a odrl-l:Power ;
    odrl-l:bearer      ex:BerlinerEnsemble ;
    odrl-l:normContent odrl:distribute ;     # content is decl(distribute); see OWL limits
    odrl-l:partOf      ex:BE-Agreement-rhoR .

ex:BE-subjection a odrl-l:Subjection ;
    odrl-l:bearer      ex:KulturPortal ;
    odrl-l:normContent odrl:distribute ;
    odrl-l:partOf      ex:BE-Agreement-rhoR .

:DeutschesTheater's agreement founds its own remedy relator, so the two providers hold distinct Powers. Which provider may demand which remedy is then fixed by the model rather than left to the implementation.

Verification

The first-order grounding the profile approximates is verified by a reproducible benchmark published with the paper's artefacts [[UFOL-PAPER]]. The benchmark contains 39 problems, each emitted as a TPTP/FOF file, an SMT-LIB 2 file, and a Turtle policy grounded in data-space entities, and checked across three independent provers plus an Isabelle/HOL mechanisation.

Entailment problems confirm the axioms are sufficient for each claimed position; discriminating problems confirm they are not too strong, witnessing the ontological distinctions an evaluator cannot express (open- vs. closed-world permission, sanctioned vs. regimented prohibition, weak vs. strong permission, and the full remedy chain). Methodology, predicate signatures, and prover-mode policy are documented with the artefacts. [[UFOL-PAPER]]
